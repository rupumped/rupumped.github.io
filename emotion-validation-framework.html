Separating Story from Feeling

Five categories of emotional communication

  • Not emotions‚Äîthese are thoughts or beliefs disguised as feelings
  • Objectively false statements that cannot be honestly validated
  • Often catastrophic or extreme interpretations
NVC: These are "faux feelings"‚Äîthoughts masquerading as emotions. True feelings are single words like angry, scared, or sad.

üó£Ô∏è "At my birthday, I feel like Alex gave me the flowers, walked over to you and spoke to you to make it seem like you were on a team, and I was on another team."
üó£Ô∏è "I feel like you're only doing this nice thing for me because you're a nice guy; not because you actually care about me specifically."
  • Mind-reading: attributing intentions or motivations to others
  • Factual claims about events that didn't happen as described
  • Interpretations presented as emotional experiences
NVC: Confuses observations with evaluations. Separating what happened from the story we tell about it is crucial.

üó£Ô∏è "I feel like I've been begging you to choose me."
üó£Ô∏è "Alex doesn't care about me."
  • Real emotions likely exist underneath, but are hidden behind interpretations
  • Claims about events (begging) or others' internal states (caring) that can't be validated
  • Closer to emotion than Category 2, but still requires rephrasing
DBT: These statements mix emotion mind with rational mind. The speaker needs to identify the actual feeling‚Äîperhaps neglected, insecure, or unimportant.

üîç Clarifying What You're Feeling: Four Diagnostic Tests

Substitution Test

Can you replace "I feel like" with "I think" without changing the meaning?

  • "I feel like I've been begging you" ‚Üí "I think I've been begging you" (same = not emotion)
  • "I feel sad" ‚Üí "I think sad" (doesn't work = emotion)

"Feeling Word" Test

After "I feel...", is the next word an actual emotion from a feelings wheel?

  • Sad, angry, scared, hurt, lonely, ashamed (validatable)
  • Like, that, as if (introduces interpretation)

Ownership Test

Is this claiming ownership of an internal experience, or making a claim about external reality?

  • "I don't feel safe with Alex" (internal experience)
  • "Alex doesn't care about me" (claim about Alex)
  • "I've been begging you" (claim about behavior)

Falsifiability Test

Could the statement theoretically be proven false with evidence?

  • "I felt unimportant" (not falsifiable)
  • "I've been begging you to choose me" (falsifiable)
  • "You didn't cancel plans" (falsifiable fact)

When a test fails, there IS an emotion there, but we need to separate the feeling from the story. Name what you observe: "You canceled our plans." Name the feeling it triggered: "I felt hurt." Notice the story you told: "You must care about them more." Separate them: "I felt hurt, and I noticed I told myself you don't value me."

‚úÖ 4. Emotion Expressed
üó£Ô∏è "Your behavior made me feel unimportant."
üó£Ô∏è "I don't feel safe in a space with Alex."
  • Actual emotions clearly stated: unimportant, unsafe, sad, angry, scared
  • Can be validated even if not grounded in objective reality
  • Feeling the emotion is a real, lived experience regardless of its basis
NVC: Clear feelings expressed. These meet the basic standard: "I feel [emotion]" with a genuine feeling word.
‚≠ê 5. Emotion Embodied
üó£Ô∏è "When you didn't immediately cancel your plans with Jordan when I told you I couldn't make it, my body reacted by increasing my heart rate and constricting my feelings. I felt sad."
üó£Ô∏è "When you choose to hang out with Alex, my body feels restless and my mind races. I feel fear."
  • Unambiguous emotion words: sad, fear, joy, anger
  • Somatic awareness: includes bodily sensations and physical responses
  • Specific behavioral trigger without blame or interpretation
  • Invites collaboration and shared problem-solving
NVC + DBT: Gold standard! Combines observation + feeling + body awareness. This is wise mind in action‚Äîintegrating emotional and rational understanding with somatic intelligence.

üí¨ What To Do When You Can't Validate

Practical responses when encountering statements from Categories 1-3

üëÅÔ∏è Affirm the subjective reality

"Something about this situation really bothered you."

Establishes that you're not dismissing their experience

‚úã State the Boundary Clearly

"I won't pretend to believe something happened the way you've interpreted it if I don't think that's accurate."

Sets a clear expectation about what you can and cannot validate

üîÑ Request a Rephrase

"When you tell yourself that story, what emotion comes up?"

Invites your partner to do the work of translating interpretation into emotion

Remember: These strategies maintain your integrity without dismissing real emotions. You're not refusing to engage. You're being curious about what would come from honest, clear communication and staying open to what you might learn about yourself in the process.
