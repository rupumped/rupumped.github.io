Introduction

The US sanctuary movement is an Underground-Railroad-inspired campaign that began in churches in the 1980s to provide safety to immigrants at risk of deportation. In the decades since, hundreds of churches across the country have participated, providing shelter and protection as well as material goods and legal resources to their guests. I had the privilege of being involved in one such campaign while I lived in Boston, and I'm proud that we successfully helped a mother and her two young daughters avoid deportation and achieve legal status.

Many organizations, including the Evangelical Lutheran Church in America and the Unitarian Universalist Association, have developed resources for congregations interested in providing sanctuary. However, few resources exist to help individual congregation members lead their communities toward participating in this mission. Before you can implement sanctuary, you must organize your community. Often, the most effective approach is not to go to your pastor first. Instead, you should borrow from the labor union playbook and engage every member of your community through one-on-one organizing conversations.

What follows is a collection of 12 example organizing conversations with members of a congregation considering whether to provide sanctuary to Maria, a community member who fled persecution in Iran. In the real world, every immigrant's story is complicated and personal, and every sanctuary congregation is unique. My hope is that these conversations can serve as a practical template for anyone working to lead their congregation to put Matthew 25:35 into practice.

Compassionate Carol, Retired Teacher, 68

Deeply moved by the member's situation and wants to help, but worried about "doing it the right way." Concerns about whether sanctuary work conflicts with "respecting the law" as a Christian principle. Needs theological grounding about civil disobedience and biblical hospitality. Once convinced, becomes a passionate advocate.

[smiling shakily] Or incredibly foolish. I guess we'll find out which. But I keep coming back to this: if not us, who? And if not now, when? Maria can't wait for ICE policies to change or for the political climate to improve. She needs sanctuary now, dangerous or not.

[firmly] Yes. Yes, I would. But let's make sure everyone in that group understands exactly what we're talking about. No illusions about safety or easy answers. If we're going to do this, we're going to do it with our eyes wide open.
